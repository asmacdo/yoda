# Nature Methods Article Requirements

Source: https://www.nature.com/nmeth/content and https://www.nature.com/nmeth/submission-guidelines/aip-and-formatting

## Structure

1. **Abstract** — up to 150 words, unreferenced
2. **Introduction** — no heading, no subheadings
3. **Results** — divided by topical subheadings
4. **Discussion** — no subheadings
5. **Online Methods** — divided by subheadings

## Limits

- Main text: 3,000 words (up to 5,000 with editorial discretion), excluding abstract, methods, references, and figure legends
- Online Methods: typically does not exceed 3,000 words
- Display items: up to 6 figures and/or tables
- References: up to 50
- Extended Data: up to 10 display items (figures and/or tables)
- Supplementary Information: permitted for material not suited to Extended Data

## LaTeX

- Use standard class files (article.cls, revtex.cls, amsart.cls, aastex.cls)
- Springer Nature LaTeX template available at https://www.springernature.com/gp/authors/campaigns/latex-author-support
- Nature applies their own style at publication
- Numerical references only (superscript in text)
- Include references in the manuscript file (no separate .bib); copy from .bbl if using BibTeX
- No footnotes permitted

## Extended Data

- Not copy-edited by Nature; authors should follow journal style closely
- Each item should fit on one page with legend/footnote below
- Must be referred to at appropriate places in main text

## References Format

- Numbered sequentially: main text, tables, figure legends, methods
- Titles required for cited articles
- URLs cited parenthetically in main text, not in reference list
- Data/code with DOIs go in reference list
- Only published/accepted articles, recognized preprints, books, and curated websites

Example:
> Eigler, D. M. & Schweizer, E. K. Positioning single atoms with a scanning tunnelling microscope. Nature 344, 524–526 (1990).

## Editorial Notes

- High priority on accessibility to non-specialists; manuscripts subject to substantial editing
- Authors encouraged to use active voice, avoid jargon and acronyms where possible
